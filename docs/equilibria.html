No. 03 · Equilibria

Where no one wants to move first.

Equilibrium is the spine of game theory — the formal account of what it means for a strategic situation to settle. This chapter walks the hierarchy: dominance, Nash, mixed strategies, subgame perfection, the folk theorem, and evolutionary stability. The math is light. The implications are not.

Chapter — III. Equilibria Reading time — ~18 min Names to know — Nash · Selten · Schelling · Maynard Smith · Axelrod
Preliminaries

What “equilibrium” actually means.

I.

An equilibrium in a game is a configuration of strategies — one for each player — such that no player can improve their own outcome by unilaterally changing their mind. That is the whole definition. It is also the source of the field’s most stubborn confusion: equilibrium is not the same thing as good. It is merely the place where the system stops moving.

The Prisoner’s Dilemma settles this in one stroke. Two suspects, each offered a deal to inform on the other, are best off if both keep quiet — but neither can afford to. Confess if your partner stays silent; you walk free. Confess if your partner confesses; you save yourself the worst sentence. Confession dominates silence row by row, column by column. The unique equilibrium is mutual betrayal. The best collective outcome — mutual silence — is on the table the entire time, and rationally unreachable.

That gap has a name. An outcome is Pareto efficient when no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off. Equilibria need not be Pareto efficient. Pareto-efficient outcomes need not be equilibria. The whole interesting machinery of the field sits in the space between these two ideas.

¶ Underlying everything is the best-response function. For each strategy your opponent might play, you have a best reply — the action that maximises your expected payoff against it. An equilibrium is a fixed point of these best-response functions: a profile where everyone is simultaneously playing their best reply to everyone else.
The easy cases

Dominance, and the strategies you should simply not play.

II.

Before invoking Nash, see how far brute reasoning will carry you. A strategy s is strictly dominated for a player if there exists another strategy s′ that yields a strictly higher payoff for that player against every possible profile of opponent strategies. A rational agent never plays a strictly dominated strategy — not because it is occasionally bad, but because something else is always better.

The Prisoner’s Dilemma is the canonical example of dominant-strategy equilibrium. Every player has a single strategy — defect — that is strictly better than the alternative against every opponent move. When all players have a dominant strategy, the equilibrium is found in a glance. No expectations. No iteration. No mixing.

When dominance is not so total, you can sometimes still solve a game by iterated elimination of strictly dominated strategies. Cross out one player’s dominated row. The remaining matrix is a smaller game; possibly something becomes dominated for the other player there. Cross it out. Repeat. If the process collapses to a single cell, that cell is the equilibrium — and it survives any plausible refinement.

Consider this 3×3. Row plays U, M, or D; Column plays L, C, or R. We’ll walk the elimination.

STEP 1 — the original game
Row picks U/M/D, Column picks L/C/R.
         L      C      R
  U   (5,2)  (1,4)  (2,1)
  M   (3,1)  (4,3)  (1,2)
  D   (1,0)  (2,2)  (3,4)

Inspect Column’s payoffs (the second coordinates). Against U: L=2, C=4, R=1. Against M: 1, 3, 2. Against D: 0, 2, 4. Column R never strictly beats both L and C, but Column L is strictly worse than C in every row (2<4, 1<3, 0<2). L is strictly dominated by C for Column.

STEP 2 — eliminate L; reconsider
The game shrinks to two columns.
         L      C      R
  U   (5,2)  (1,4)  (2,1)
  M   (3,1)  (4,3)  (1,2)
  D   (1,0)  (2,2)  (3,4)

In the reduced game, look at Row’s first coordinates. U gives (1, 2); M gives (4, 1); D gives (2, 3). U is strictly dominated by D for Row (1<2 and 2<3). M and D each win one column; neither dominates the other. Eliminate U.

STEP 3 — one more pass
Collapse to a single cell.
         C      R
  U   (1,4)  (2,1)
  M   (4,3)  (1,2)
  D   (2,2)  (3,4)

Now for Column: C gives (3, 2) against (M, D); R gives (2, 4). Against D, R beats C (4>2); against M, C beats R. No dominance — we need best responses. Row prefers M against C (4>2) and D against R (3>1). Column prefers C against M (3>2) and R against D (4>2). The unique fixed point is (D, R). Equilibrium reached.

Iterated elimination did most of the work; the final step closed with a best-response check. A small caution: the procedure above used only strict dominance, which is order-free — the same equilibrium survives whichever dominated strategy you remove first. Weak dominance is the cousin you should treat with suspicion. A strategy is weakly dominated if some alternative is at least as good against every opponent action and strictly better against at least one. Iterated weak dominance can change the surviving set depending on the elimination order. Use it sparingly, and check the answer.

The central concept

Nash equilibrium.

III.

Most games are not solvable by dominance alone. For the rest we have the Nash equilibrium: a strategy profile in which every player’s strategy is a best response to the strategies the others are playing. Nobody, knowing what everyone else is doing, has any reason to deviate. John Nash introduced the concept in his 1950 Princeton dissertation and proved — in a famously short 1951 paper in the Annals of Mathematics — that every finite game has at least one such equilibrium, provided we permit randomization.

For a finite game with pure strategies alone, equilibria are easy to find by hand. Mark each player’s best response to each of the opponent’s actions; wherever both players’ marks coincide in a cell, that cell is a pure-strategy Nash equilibrium. Stag Hunt has two such cells; mark them and see.

Stag Hunt makes the selection problem visible. When a game has multiple equilibria, the theory itself does not tell you which one will be played. Stag-Stag is unambiguously better — it is the payoff-dominant equilibrium — but Hare-Hare is the risk-dominant one: if you have no idea what your partner will do, hunting hare guarantees you a hare. Real populations sometimes coordinate on the payoff-dominant outcome and sometimes do not. The math is silent on which.

Battle of the Sexes (treated on the foundations page) is the same problem in a more pointed costume: two pure equilibria, both preferred by one party. Schelling’s answer — that real players find their way to a focal point, an outcome that is conspicuous for reasons outside the payoff matrix — remains the most plausible account of how the selection problem is solved in practice.

What Nash equilibrium does not promise

Existence is not uniqueness. Uniqueness is not realism. The concept assumes common knowledge of rationality and payoffs — a fierce assumption that Robert Aumann spent a career formalising. Bounded rationality, learning, partial information, and outright miscoordination all live outside the basic framework. Nash equilibrium is a benchmark for analysis. It is rarely a prediction.

Randomisation

Mixed strategies and the indifference principle.

IV.

Some games have no pure-strategy Nash equilibrium. Matching Pennies is the classical proof. Each player chooses Heads or Tails simultaneously; if the coins match, Row wins a unit from Column; if they differ, Column wins a unit from Row. Whichever side Row anticipates Column will play, Row has a strict incentive to choose the opposite; Column then wants to switch in response. Pure strategies chase each other forever.

Nash’s 1951 theorem demands we admit randomisation. A mixed strategy is a probability distribution over the pure strategies. Once mixed strategies are permitted, every finite game has at least one Nash equilibrium. Some have only mixed ones.

Finding a mixed equilibrium is a tidy exercise. It rests on the indifference principle: if a player is mixing — assigning positive probability to two or more pure strategies — she must be indifferent between them given the opponent’s mix. If one pure action gave a higher expected payoff than another, she would simply play the better one and stop mixing. So in equilibrium, the opponent’s mix is chosen precisely to keep her indifferent.

For Matching Pennies, let Column play Heads with probability q. Row’s expected payoff from Heads is q(1) + (1−q)(−1) = 2q − 1. From Tails it is q(−1) + (1−q)(1) = 1 − 2q. Set them equal — the indifference condition — and solve.

Indifference for Row 2q − 1 = 1 − 2q ⇒ 4q = 2 ⇒ q = 1/2 By symmetry, Row plays Heads with p = 1/2. The unique Nash equilibrium is (1/2, 1/2) on both sides.

Battle of the Sexes adds a third equilibrium on top of its two pure ones. Suppose Alex prefers Opera (3 if both go to Opera, 1 if both go to Boxing) and Sam prefers Boxing (3 to Opera’s 2). Let Alex play Opera with probability p; Sam plays Opera with probability q. Sam is indifferent when Alex’s mix makes Sam’s expected payoff from Opera equal to that from Boxing.

Sam indifferent — solve for p 2p + 0(1−p) = 0·p + 3(1−p) ⇒ 2p = 3 − 3p ⇒ p = 3/5 Alex indifferent — by symmetry q = 2/5

So a third equilibrium exists: Alex goes to Opera 3/5 of the time; Sam goes to Opera 2/5 of the time. The expected payoff is lower than either pure equilibrium for both players — a perverse outcome that can nevertheless persist when neither side can credibly commit. Schelling’s instinct here is corrective: in the real world people find a focal point — the venue from last year, the one whose tickets are already bought — and the mixed equilibrium is mostly a theoretical curiosity. The point of the math is to show what would happen if no such focal point existed.

¶ Aumann’s 1976 paper “Agreeing to Disagree” introduced common knowledge as the formal underpinning of equilibrium reasoning. If each player knows the others are rational, and knows that they know, and knows that they know that they know, all the way down — then equilibria become coherent predictions. Remove any layer of that tower and the theory begins to wobble.
Sequential play

Extensive-form games and the problem of credibility.

V.

So far the games have been one-shot, simultaneous, drawn on a single matrix. Many real situations are sequential: someone moves, the other responds, the first moves again. These are extensive-form games, drawn as trees. Nash equilibrium applies to them — but a peculiar pathology emerges. A Nash equilibrium can rest on a threat that the threatener would never actually carry out.

The textbook case is entry deterrence. A monopolist incumbent faces a potential entrant. The entrant chooses to enter the market or stay out. If the entrant stays out, the incumbent enjoys monopoly profits. If the entrant enters, the incumbent chooses to accommodate (split the market) or fight (a price war that hurts both, but the entrant more). The incumbent threatens, before the fact, to fight any entry. If the entrant believes the threat, she stays out. Mutual happiness for the incumbent.

Apply Nash equilibrium naively and you get two equilibria. In one, the entrant stays out; the incumbent commits to fight. Nobody actually deviates — the entrant doesn’t enter, so the incumbent never has to act on the threat — so the profile is technically Nash. But it depends on a threat the incumbent would prefer to abandon if put to the test. Once entry occurs, fighting yields 1 and accommodating yields 4. The threat to fight is, in Reinhard Selten’s phrase, not credible.

Selten’s 1965 paper introduced subgame-perfect equilibrium (SPE) to fix this. An SPE is a Nash equilibrium of the whole game whose strategy profile, restricted to any subgame, is still a Nash equilibrium of that subgame. The procedure for finding one is backward induction: solve the tree from the leaves backward. At each decision node, the moving player chooses the action with the highest payoff, given the equilibrium play in the subgames below. Substitute that payoff for the node. Repeat until you reach the root.

In entry deterrence, backward induction kills the non-credible threat. Knowing the incumbent will accommodate, the entrant enters. The unique SPE is (Enter, Accommodate) and the monopolist’s threat is exposed as theatre.

Two refinements deserve a sentence each. Trembling-hand perfection, also from Selten, requires that the equilibrium be robust to vanishingly small probabilities of mistaken moves — equilibria that depend on perfect play break down. Sequential equilibrium, due to David Kreps and Robert Wilson (1982), extends the logic to games of incomplete information, requiring that players’ beliefs about hidden moves be consistent with Bayes’ rule everywhere the rule applies. Both refinements push the Nash concept closer to predicting actual behaviour. Neither closes the gap entirely.

When the game repeats

Repeated play, the folk theorem, and Tit-for-Tat.

VI.

One round of the Prisoner’s Dilemma ends in mutual defection. A hundred rounds — or an indefinite number, with a positive probability of continuing each time — can end almost anywhere. Repetition is not a small tweak. It rewrites the game.

The result is known, in characteristic informality, as the folk theorem: it was understood by the community before anyone troubled to publish it. In its sharpest form, due to Drew Fudenberg and Eric Maskin (1986), the theorem says that in an infinitely repeated game with sufficiently patient players, any feasible payoff profile that gives each player at least their minimax value can be sustained as a subgame-perfect equilibrium. The set of equilibria explodes. Cooperation becomes possible. So does almost anything else.

The mechanism is punishment. A player cooperates today because defecting triggers retaliation tomorrow, and the discounted cost of that retaliation outweighs the one-shot gain. Trigger strategies formalise this. Grim trigger is the harshest: cooperate so long as the opponent has cooperated in every previous round; defect forever after any defection. The threat is enormous and (because grim trigger itself is a best response in the punishment phase) credible. Tit-for-Tat is its forgiving cousin: cooperate on the first move; afterwards do whatever the opponent did last move. Defections are punished once, and only once.

In 1980, Robert Axelrod ran a computer tournament that has since become folklore. He invited researchers to submit programs to play 200 rounds of the iterated Prisoner’s Dilemma against every other entry, round-robin. The winner, submitted by the mathematician and peace researcher Anatol Rapoport, was a four-line BASIC program implementing Tit-for-Tat. Axelrod ran the tournament a second time, with the first-round results published. Tit-for-Tat won again.

From the results Axelrod extracted four traits common to the successful strategies. They were nice — never the first to defect. They were retaliatory — never let a defection slide. They were forgiving — willing to restart cooperation after a single round of punishment. And they were clear — simple enough that an opponent could read their pattern and learn to cooperate. The strategies that lost were typically clever and unpredictable.

Why the folk theorem matters

Cooperation in repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma is not a moral fact about humans or a miracle of altruism. It is a mathematical consequence of the shadow of the future: when the next round is likely, and when punishment is credible, mutual cooperation becomes individually rational. Remove either condition — shorten the game, make players impatient, weaken the ability to detect or punish defection — and the depressing equilibrium returns.

The mechanism works only when players can recognise each other across encounters and when the future is weighted heavily enough against the present (in the formal model, a discount factor close enough to 1). Strip those conditions away and the folk theorem still applies, but the only sustainable equilibria collapse back toward the one-shot result. Strategies without strategists

Evolutionary stability.

VII.

Game theory was built for rational agents weighing options. Biology has neither. John Maynard Smith and George Price adapted the framework in the early 1970s by stripping out the rationality assumption and replacing it with differential reproduction. Strategies that yield higher payoffs become more common because their bearers have more offspring. Selection plays the role of deliberation.

The central concept is the evolutionarily stable strategy, or ESS. A strategy is an ESS if, once it is common in a population, no rare mutant strategy can invade and outcompete it. Formally, strategy s is an ESS if for any alternative s′, either (i) the payoff of s against itself is strictly greater than the payoff of s′ against s, or (ii) those payoffs are equal but s does strictly better against s′ than s′ does against itself. Every ESS is a Nash equilibrium. Not every Nash equilibrium is an ESS.

Hawk-Dove is the canonical biological model. Two animals contest a resource of value V. Hawks fight, sometimes winning, sometimes taking injury of cost C. Doves display and retreat. When C > V, neither pure Hawk nor pure Dove is evolutionarily stable: a Dove population is invaded by Hawks (who steal resources unopposed) and a Hawk population is invaded by Doves (who avoid the injurious fights). The ESS is a mixed population — or, equivalently, a mixed strategy of playing Hawk with probability V/C.

The dynamics by which a population approaches an ESS go by the name replicator dynamic: the share of each strategy grows in proportion to how much that strategy’s payoff exceeds the population average. The replicator equation is the simplest model of strategic learning, and it appears far outside biology — in models of multi-agent reinforcement learning, in cultural transmission, in industrial competition. When a learning rule rewards better-performing strategies, replicator-like dynamics emerge for free.

Evolutionary game theory is the bridge from rational-choice theory to a wider set of explanations — for animal conflict, for the cultural drift of norms, for the way artificial agents trained against one another converge on stable policies. It tells us that equilibrium is not only the destination of careful thinkers. It is the destination of any system that copies what works.

Putting it together

A hierarchy of solutions.

VIII.

The concepts in this chapter nest. Dominant-strategy equilibria, where they exist, are also Nash. Nash equilibria are the universe within which everything else operates. Subgame-perfect equilibria are Nash equilibria that survive the credibility test in every subgame. Evolutionarily stable strategies are Nash equilibria that survive invasion by mutants. Refinements narrow; they never widen.

The history is shorter than the framework. Nash gave us existence in 1950–51. Aumann formalised the common-knowledge assumptions in the 1970s and won a Nobel for them in 2005. Selten introduced subgame perfection in 1965 — the work that earned him a Nobel alongside Nash and Harsanyi in 1994. Maynard Smith took the apparatus into biology in 1973. Schelling spent four decades arguing, with characteristic charm, that the math was only the first half of the answer; the rest was a question of focal points, social conventions, and what the room expected. Axelrod showed, with a tournament, that the simplest decent rule often outperforms the cleverest.
