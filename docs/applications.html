No. 04 · Applications

From blackboard to boardroom.

Game theory will not hand you the right answer. What it will do — if you let it — is discipline the question. This volume is about taking that discipline outside the seminar room: into pricing meetings, salary negotiations, evolutionary biology, machine-learning labs, and whose turn it is to do the dishes.

Thesis

Discipline, not prediction.

I.

Game theory does not predict; it disciplines. It forces you, before you do anything else, to (a) name the players, (b) enumerate the strategies each one actually has, (c) put numbers — or at least an ordering — on the payoffs, and then (d) ask the only question that matters: what is my best response to what they will do? A grandmaster does not compute Nash equilibria over the board. She does, however, think about what her opponent will think she will think. The mathematics is a scaffolding for that habit of mind, nothing more and nothing less.

The strategic thinker’s pocket questions

  1. Who else is in this game — including the ones not at the table?
  2. What options does each of them actually have, not the ones I wish they had?
  3. What do they want? What is their utility, in their currency, not mine?
  4. What do they think I will do? And what do they think I think they will do?
  5. What is my best response to that? Is there a move that survives whatever they pick?

Five questions, asked honestly, will catch most of the strategic mistakes a smart person otherwise makes. The rest of this volume is examples.

In the firm

Business strategy and competition.

II.
Quantity vs. price

Cournot, Bertrand, and the road to marginal cost.

Two firms competing on quantity — the Cournot model — settle into a tolerably profitable duopoly. Two firms competing on price for an identical product — Bertrand — cut each other to marginal cost in one round and stay there. The lesson is tactical. If your product is genuinely a commodity, you are in Bertrand, and your margin is whatever scrap of differentiation, switching cost, or capacity constraint you can defend. Most strategic memos are, underneath, about getting out of Bertrand.

Threats

Entry deterrence and what makes a threat credible.

An incumbent threatens to flood the market if a rival enters. Is the threat credible? Only if carrying it out is in the incumbent’s interest after the rival has already entered — what theorists call subgame perfection. Sunk capacity makes the threat real: if you have already built the factory, undercutting is cheap. A reputation built across many markets makes it real: bleeding once in market A to deter entry into B, C, and D pays. Press-release threats made by executives who could quietly accommodate are almost always cheap talk.

Coopetition

When to compete; when to grow the pie.

Adam Brandenburger and Barry Nalebuff, in Co-opetition (1996), pointed out that most business situations are not zero-sum: a firm’s rivals are sometimes its complements. Intel and AMD compete fiercely on price and yet defend the x86 instruction set together. Airlines wage capacity wars on routes and share alliances on revenue. Knowing which game you are in — pie-splitting or pie-growing — is half the strategy.

¶ Supermarket price-matching guarantees read like consumer-friendly policies. They are also commitment devices: by promising to undercut any competitor, a chain credibly removes its own incentive to start a price war, since the rival’s cut will be matched automatically. The threat is now both ways, and equilibrium prices stay high. Schelling would have admired the construction.
Mechanism design

Auctions and the design of markets.

III.

Four canonical auction formats cover most of what you will ever encounter. They look different on the floor; they are deeply related on the page.

Fig. 1 — The four classical auction formats, contrasted.
Format How it runs Optimal bid for you Seen in
English (open ascending) Bids rise openly; last bidder standing wins and pays the final bid. Stay in while price < your value; drop the instant it exceeds it. Sotheby’s, eBay (effectively).
Dutch (open descending) Clock starts high and falls; first bidder to call buys at the called price. Call before the price reaches the next competitor’s expected jump-in. Aalsmeer flower market; some IPO allocations.
First-price sealed-bid Each bidder submits one sealed bid; highest wins and pays their own bid. Shade below your value — pay less than you would have paid. Construction contracts, government tenders.
Second-price sealed-bid (Vickrey) Highest sealed bid wins; pays the second-highest bid. Bid your true value. No shading. Truthful Google/Meta ad auctions (in spirit); spectrum sales.

Revenue equivalence (Vickrey, 1961; Myerson, 1981): under symmetric, risk-neutral, independent private values, all four yield the same expected revenue. In the messy real world they don’t — which is why design matters.

Vickrey’s insight

Why second-price auctions reward honesty.

In a sealed second-price auction, bidding your true value is a dominant strategy — the best move regardless of what anyone else does. Bid lower and you risk losing an auction you would have happily won; bid higher and you risk winning above what the item is worth. The price you pay is set by the runner-up, so your own bid changes only whether you win, not what you pay. The proof is two lines; William Vickrey got a Nobel for it in 1996. Google and Meta’s ad auctions are second-price in spirit for the same reason: advertisers stop guessing what to bid and start telling the system what a click is worth.

Concrete moves at an auction

If you are selling and bidders’ values are independent, prefer a second-price (Vickrey) format and set a serious reserve price — the reserve, not the bidders, is doing most of the work for your revenue. If you are bidding in a second-price auction, write down your true value and submit it. In a first-price auction, shade below your value by a margin that grows with the number of likely bidders. In any common-value auction — oil tracts, M&A targets, blind spectrum — assume that winning means you overestimated, and shade further still.

The winner’s curse

Why winning a common-value auction is bad news.

If everyone is bidding for an oil tract whose true value is unknown but the same for all of you, the bidder who wins is — almost by definition — the one who overestimated it most. This is the winner’s curse. The remedy is to shade your bid downward by the expected overestimation conditional on winning. The deeper lesson: when the asset is more uncertain, bid lower, not higher. M&A bankers re-learn this every cycle.

Reverse game theory

Mechanism design: write the rules so rationality picks the right answer.

Standard game theory: take the rules, find equilibrium. Mechanism design: take the equilibrium you want and reverse-engineer the rules. Alvin Roth’s kidney-exchange programs match donors and recipients so nobody profits by defecting from the chain. The Gale–Shapley deferred-acceptance algorithm (1962) underpins the U.S. medical residency match and a generation of school-choice systems, because under it students cannot do better by lying about their preferences. The 1994 FCC spectrum auction, designed by economists who had read all of the above, raised roughly $20 billion earlier designs would have left on the table.

At the table

Negotiation and bargaining.

IV.

Nash, before he proved his equilibrium theorem, gave us the Nash bargaining solution (1950): when two parties split a surplus, they will divide it in proportion to how much each one would lose by walking away. Your share of the pie grows with two things and only two things — what you bring to the table, and what you can credibly do without it.

BATNA

Your alternative is your power.

Roger Fisher and William Ury, in Getting to Yes (1981), named the most useful concept in negotiation: your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. You will not get more from this negotiation than you can credibly walk away to. Improving your BATNA before the meeting — another offer, another supplier, another tenant — raises your floor more reliably than any rhetorical trick at the table. Most amateur negotiators try to win the conversation. Professionals improve the alternatives.

Commitment

Schelling: burn the bridge, win the war.

Thomas Schelling’s deepest contribution to bargaining is the paradoxical observation that limiting your own options can improve your position. A general who burns the bridge behind his troops thereby tells the enemy he will fight. A buyer who has publicly told her board she will not pay more than $X has raised her bargaining floor. To commit credibly, you must make the commitment costly to reverse; otherwise it is talk.

Tactical moves you can use this week

Anchor first if you know the market better than the other side — the first number stuck in someone’s head is the one their counter-offers will orbit. If you know less, let them anchor. Ask for the “no” before the “yes”: “Would it be unreasonable to…?” makes refusal feel like a concession. Never bargain against yourself — if your last offer is met with silence, the next move is theirs, not yours. And always be willing to walk; the moment you cannot, you have already lost.

At scale

Public goods, coordination, and politics.

V.
Free-riding

Why nobody volunteers to clean the kitchen.

A public good is non-rival and non-excludable: clean air, climate stability, herd immunity, the open-source library you use but never contribute to. The individual incentive is to free-ride and let others bear the cost — which, when everyone reasons that way, is why the kitchen stays dirty and the atmosphere keeps warming. Solutions all run through the same handful of moves: shrink the group until reputation bites, introduce excludability (paywalls, club goods), introduce enforcement (taxes, treaties with teeth), or change the payoffs so private interest aligns with the public one.

Voting

Condorcet, Arrow, and the strategic ballot.

Three candidates, three voters with different preferences: it is perfectly possible that A beats B in a pairwise vote, B beats C, and C beats A. This is the Condorcet paradox, known since 1785. Kenneth Arrow’s impossibility theorem (1951) is the brutal generalization: no voting rule satisfying a handful of innocuous-sounding fairness conditions can aggregate ranked preferences from three or more options without producing pathologies. The honest implication is that voting your conscience — ranking your true favorite first — is itself a strategic choice with consequences. In a first-past-the-post race with three candidates and only one you can stomach, sometimes the equilibrium is to vote for your second choice. That is not cynicism; it is arithmetic.

¶ Polarization is, in game-theoretic terms, a coordination failure on top of a signalling game. When the cost of moderate signals rises and the reward for extreme signals does too — in primaries, in cable news, in social-media feeds — the rational individual move drifts to the poles even when no individual prefers the resulting equilibrium. The math does not blame anyone. It just predicts the result.
International relations

Deterrence as a repeated game.

Schelling’s The Strategy of Conflict (1960) read as an instruction manual for the nuclear age in Washington and Moscow alike. Mutual assured destruction is Chicken with very large payoffs and very long memories. Arms-control treaties — ABM, START, NPT — are commitment devices: by making certain moves costly to walk back, they shrink the strategy space on both sides. The same machinery is at work today in cyber, trade, and AI export controls. The players change. The shape of the game does not.

In the wild

Evolution, biology, and the selfish gene.

VI.

Game theory’s most surprising export was into biology. John Maynard Smith and George Price, in 1973, asked what proportion of aggressive (Hawk) and peaceable (Dove) animals would persist in a population where genes did whatever propagated them best. They proved that a mixed equilibrium exists — an evolutionarily stable strategy, or ESS — and wrote down its payoff matrix.

With V=4 and C=8: a pure population of Hawks injures itself to death; a pure population of Doves is a buffet for the first mutant Hawk. Neither extreme is stable. What survives is the frequency of Hawks that makes the two strategies indifferent to each other — V/C, here one-half. No one chose this. Selection found it.

Reciprocity

Vampire bats, cleaner fish, and the gene’s long memory.

Robert Trivers’s 1971 paper on reciprocal altruism showed how cooperation can evolve in unrelated individuals: in a repeated interaction, the gene that says help today, expect help tomorrow, punish defectors outcompetes the gene that says take what you can. Vampire bats regurgitate blood for hungry roost-mates and remember who reciprocated. Cleaner fish service larger fish that, by all selfish logic, ought to eat them — and don’t, because the relationship is repeated. W. D. Hamilton’s rule (rb > c: help relatives when relatedness times benefit exceeds cost) does the same work for kin. Cooperation is not the opposite of selfishness; under the right structure, it is what selfishness produces. Most human norms — queueing, tipping, reciprocated favours — are learned strategies playing the role evolution plays for the bat.

Silicon players

AI and algorithmic agents.

VII.

Classical theory imagined one rational agent computing equilibria against another. Modern AI imagines two neural networks learning simultaneously against each other — multi-agent reinforcement learning. The fixed point of training is not a hand-derived Nash equilibrium but an empirical one: agents stop updating when neither can improve unilaterally. The math is familiar; the agents are not.

Alignment

The principal-agent problem, reborn.

An AI system trained by a principal who cannot fully observe its reasoning is, structurally, a principal-agent problem with asymmetric information. The economic literature on moral hazard, screening, and signalling is not optional reading for AI safety; it is the prior art.

Algorithmic collusion

When pricing bots discover cartels on their own.

Emilio Calvano and colleagues showed in 2020 that even simple Q-learning pricing agents, with no ability to communicate, can converge on supra-competitive prices in repeated interaction. They appear to learn something like Tit-for-Tat over price levels: undercut me and I punish, raise prices and I follow. No phone call, no smoky room — just two algorithms playing a repeated game and finding the cooperative equilibrium that human cartels famously struggle to sustain. The regulatory implications are unresolved, and getting louder.

Mechanism design for AI

Designing the arena so the bots play well.

Every algorithmic marketplace — ad auctions, recommender systems, decentralized exchanges, prediction markets — is a designed game. The rules determine the equilibrium. The interesting frontier is no longer training a single agent; it is designing the rules under which many of them will find an equilibrium you can live with.

Small stakes

Everyday life, played well.

VIII.

None of this is theoretical. The same machinery shows up in the choices you make before lunch.

  • Splitting the bill. With strangers, itemize — one-shot game, free-riding pays. With friends you will see next week, split evenly and accept the variance; the repeated game punishes pettiness.
  • Salary negotiation. Don’t name a number first unless you have better information than the recruiter. If asked: cite a range anchored on independently sourced data, and make the floor of that range your real target.
  • House hunting. Write down your reservation price before you walk through the property. The kitchen will not get less charming over the next hour, but your floor will, unless you have pinned it in advance.
  • Whose turn to do the dishes. Don’t play it as a one-shot. Establish a tit-for-tat rule (yesterday me, today you) and let the repetition do the enforcement. Norms beat negotiations.
  • Online discourse. The classic mistake is treating the timeline as a one-shot game with strangers. Most online communities are repeated games with reputation; what you post today will be quoted at you in 2027. Bid accordingly.
  • Picking a lane in traffic. The fastest lane is, in equilibrium, no faster than yours; if it were, drivers would already have switched. Stop switching. The variance costs more than the mean buys.
Bibliography

A short reading list to go deeper.

IX.

Canonical further reading.

  • Thomas C. Schelling — The Strategy of Conflict (1960) and Micromotives and Macrobehavior (1978). Commitment, deterrence, and the social arithmetic of small choices that aggregate into big patterns.
  • Robert Axelrod — The Evolution of Cooperation (1984). The Tit-for-Tat tournament, and the conditions under which nice, retaliatory, forgiving, and clear beats clever.
  • Avinash Dixit & Barry Nalebuff — The Art of Strategy (2008). The best general-audience introduction; case studies from business, sports, and politics, lightly mathematical.
  • Roger Fisher & William Ury — Getting to Yes (1981). BATNA, principled negotiation, and how to separate the people from the problem.
  • Adam Brandenburger & Barry Nalebuff — Co-opetition (1996). When competitors are also complements; how to grow the pie before fighting over slices.
  • Alvin E. Roth — Who Gets What — and Why (2015). Market design from the inside: kidney exchanges, school choice, medical residencies.
  • John Maynard Smith — Evolution and the Theory of Games (1982). How game theory became biology’s second language; ESS, Hawk-Dove, and beyond.
  • Drew Fudenberg & Jean Tirole — Game Theory (1991). The graduate textbook. Read it when you are ready for the proofs.
Coda

The world does not owe you equilibrium.

X.

Game theory disciplines your thinking; it does not replace it. Name the players. List the strategies. Estimate the payoffs — even badly. Ask what they will do in reply to what you do in reply to what they do. The math is the scaffolding for that habit. Most strategic failure, in business and in life, is failure to do that work; most strategic success is having done it before anyone else thought to.

The world does not owe you a Nash equilibrium. You have to find one — or design one — and then make it stable enough to live in.
