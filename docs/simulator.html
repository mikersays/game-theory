No. 05 · The Tournament

Run Axelrod’s tournament yourself.

In 1980, Robert Axelrod invited the world’s game theorists to submit strategies for the iterated Prisoner’s Dilemma. He pitted every entry against every other for 200 rounds apiece. Anatol Rapoport’s four-line Tit-for-Tat won. Here is that tournament — in your browser, with knobs — so you can watch the same surprising lesson play out in real time.

Preliminaries

The game, in one paragraph.

I.

Each round, both players choose simultaneously between cooperate (C) and defect (D). Mutual cooperation pays the reward R to each. Mutual defection pays the punishment P. A lone defector earns the temptation T at the cooperator’s expense, who receives the sucker’s payoff S. The canonical numbers are T = 5, R = 3, P = 1, S = 0; the canonical inequality is T > R > P > S, with the further constraint 2R > T + S so that mutual cooperation strictly beats alternating exploitation. Played once, the rational move is to defect — and both players know it. Played a hundred times against the same opponent, with memory and reputation, the answer is wildly less obvious. That is the whole point of the exercise.

¶ Axelrod ran the tournament twice. The second time everyone had read the analysis of the first. Tit-for-Tat won again. There is no greater compliment a strategy can pay than to be re-submitted, in better company, and still come first.

II.

Pick one strategy to be “yours.” The simulator will play your strategy — and every other strategy in the pool — against every other strategy, itself included. After the dust settles you get a leaderboard, a horizontal bar chart, and a clear answer to the only question that matters: how would you have done?

III.

The leaderboard hides the texture. Pull any two strategies apart, watch them play move by move, and you can see exactly how a tournament outcome arises: where the first defection lands, who breaks the détente, whether anyone is forgiving enough to climb back out. Run Tit-for-Tat against Always Defect and watch a single cooperative move get punished into oblivion. Then run Tit-for-Two-Tats against an alternating defector and watch it get fleeced.

What to notice

Four things the tournament will teach you.

IV.

Axelrod squeezed his data for moral lessons, and they have aged better than most empirical findings in social science. Run the simulator a few times — with and without noise, with short and long matches — and watch these four show up unbidden.

Nice strategies finish near the top.

Strategies that never defect first — ALLC, TFT, TF2T, GTFT, GRIM, PAV — tend to cluster at the head of the leaderboard. The reason is mechanical: they get the mutual-cooperation reward against each other and against themselves, which dominates everything else.

Pure cooperation is suicidal.

Always Cooperate scores zero against Always Defect, every time. Niceness without the capacity to retaliate is just a buffet. Tit-for-Tat’s genius is that it punishes immediately and forgives immediately — it loses one round, never more, against an unrepentant defector.

Forgiveness pays under noise.

Turn the noise slider up to 5 or 10 percent and re-run. Two Tit-for-Tats fall into a recriminatory loop on the first stray defection; two Generous Tit-for-Tats forgive their way back to cooperation. The world is noisy; durable cooperation has to be.

Clarity beats cleverness.

The simple strategies — four lines of code — outperform the elaborate ones. Opponents can read them. Predictability is a feature, not a bug. The strategies that fail are usually the ones that try to be too clever, and end up confusing themselves.

“What accounts for Tit-for-Tat’s robust success is its combination of being nice, retaliatory, forgiving, and clear. Its niceness prevents it from getting into unnecessary trouble. Its retaliation discourages the other side from persisting whenever defection is tried. Its forgiveness helps restore mutual cooperation. And its clarity makes it intelligible to the other player, thereby eliciting long-term cooperation.” Robert Axelrod, The Evolution of Cooperation, 1984

Where this leads.

V.

The simulator is a microcosm of one of the deepest results in the theory: the folk theorem for repeated games. In a one-shot Prisoner’s Dilemma, mutual defection is the only equilibrium. Stretch the game indefinitely — or even just give players a sufficiently high probability of meeting again — and almost any outcome, including full cooperation, can be sustained as an equilibrium. The shadow of the future does the work.

For the formal treatment — subgame perfection, trigger strategies, the discount factor and what it really represents — carry on to Equilibria. For a deeper case study of where these dynamics show up outside the lab — in arms races, climate negotiations, OPEC, prison-cell informants — see Applications.
