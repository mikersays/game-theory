No. 01 · Introduction

The arithmetic of other people’s minds.

Game theory is the mathematics of how rational agents make decisions when outcomes depend on the choices of others. It is the study of strategy where there is no “right” answer in isolation — only answers conditional on what the rest of the room will do.

Subject — Strategic decision-making Founded — 1944, Princeton Authors — von Neumann · Morgenstern · Nash · Schelling
Prologue

A short field briefing.

I.

Most of what we call “strategy” — in business, war, romance, politics, evolution — is really one problem in different costumes: how should I act when the outcome of my action depends on what someone else, also reasoning, decides to do? A chess player has it. A central banker has it. So does the bacterium choosing whether to spend energy on a virulence gene.

In 1944, the polymath John von Neumann and the economist Oskar Morgenstern published Theory of Games and Economic Behavior and gave the problem its modern language: players, strategies, payoffs, equilibrium. Six years later, a 22-year-old John Nash proved that under mild conditions every finite game has at least one configuration in which no player can do better by unilaterally changing their mind. We have been chasing the implications ever since.

Game theory will not tell you what to want. It assumes you already know that — your preferences, your utility. What it does, with merciless economy, is tell you what a clear-eyed opponent who also knows your preferences will do, and therefore what you ought to do in reply. Sometimes the answer is uncomfortable. Sometimes it is that two intelligent people, each acting perfectly rationally, will produce an outcome both consider worse than another they could have agreed to. That is not a bug. It is, often, the point.

¶ Thomas Schelling, whose The Strategy of Conflict (1960) turned game theory toward the Cold War, liked to remind students that “one cannot, without empirical evidence, deduce what understandings can be perceived in a non-zero-sum game of maneuver.” In other words: the math gives you a frame. People still have to live inside it.

II.

A handful of small, abstract games keep recurring — in arms races, in office politics, in mating displays. Each one is a different shape of incentive. Learn the shapes and you start to see them everywhere.

Game No. 01

Prisoner’s Dilemma

Each player gains by defecting whatever the other does — yet both are worse off when both defect. Cooperation is collectively rational, individually unstable.

In the wild

Where it actually shows up.

III.

Game theory is rarely the headline. It is the grammar underneath the headline — the reason the spectrum auction raised $20 billion, why your airline ticket is priced the way it is, why two peacocks waste energy on tail feathers, why the climate negotiation keeps stalling at the same impasse.

Pricing & Auctions

Vickrey, English, Dutch, sealed-bid. The FCC’s 1994 spectrum auction was designed by economists who had read Vickrey — and it printed money.

Antitrust & Cartels

OPEC, airlines, the lysine cartel. Every long-running price-fix is a repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma where defection is one phone call away.

Evolutionary Biology

Maynard Smith’s evolutionary stable strategies explain hawks and doves, sex ratios, and why most species don’t kill rivals outright.

Climate & Public Goods

Every emissions treaty is a Stag Hunt at planetary scale. The math is friendly. The credible commitments are hard.

Politics & Voting

Arrow’s impossibility, median-voter theorems, strategic voting. Why two-party systems converge to the center — until they don’t.

AI & Alignment

Multi-agent RL, mechanism design for LLMs, the principal-agent problem reborn as specification gaming.

Sports & Mixed Strategies

Penalty kicks, serve-and-return in tennis, play-calling in football. The optimum is almost never predictable.

Everyday Life

Splitting rent, choosing a lane, ending a Zoom call, asking for a raise. Most of it is bargaining under incomplete information.

A famous result

Why nice, forgiving, and provocable wins.

IV.

In 1980, the political scientist Robert Axelrod ran a tournament. He invited researchers to submit computer programs to play 200 rounds of the iterated Prisoner’s Dilemma against every other entry. The winner, submitted by Anatol Rapoport, was almost embarrassingly short: cooperate on the first move; afterward, do whatever the opponent did last move. Four lines of BASIC. It is called Tit-for-Tat.

Axelrod ran the tournament again, with the strategies known. Tit-for-Tat won the second one too. From the results he extracted a moral that has aged remarkably well: successful strategies are nice (they never defect first), retaliatory (they never let a defection slide), forgiving (they don’t hold grudges), and clear (the opponent can tell what you’re doing).

The Axelrod conditions for cooperation

Cooperation can emerge in a population of self-interested agents if (a) interactions are repeated, (b) the future is sufficiently weighted against the present, and (c) players can recognize each other across encounters. Strip any one of these away and you tend to get the depressing equilibrium back.

“The Tit-for-Tat strategy demonstrates that there is a single rule which, although simple, is surprisingly robust across a wide variety of strategic environments.” Robert Axelrod, The Evolution of Cooperation, 1984
